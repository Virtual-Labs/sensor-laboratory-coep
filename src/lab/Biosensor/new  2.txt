<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 13.5pt; line-height: 150%;"><span style='font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: "Verdana","sans-serif"; color: black;'>For measurement of Temperature number of sensors are available. One of the most linear, stable, and reproducible temperature sensors is the RTD, Resistance Temperature Detector. In RTD, the  output resistance changes with temperature. RTD is a positive temperature coefficient device. The resistance of the metal increases with temperature. The resistive property of the metal is called its resistivity. The resistive property defines length and cross sectional area required to fabricate an RTD of a given value. The resistance is proportional to length and inversely proportional to the cross sectional area and is given by the relationship</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 13.5pt; line-height: 150%; text-align: justify;"><span style='font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: "Verdana","sans-serif"; color: black;'>Theoretically, any metal can be used to measure temperature. The metal selected should have a high melting point and an ability to withstand the effects of corrosion. T</span><span style='font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: "Verdana","sans-serif"; color: black;'>herefore </span><span style='font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: "Verdana","sans-serif"; color: black;'>Platinum is  selected for most of the applications. Its desirable characteristics include chemical stability, availability in a pure form, and electrical properties that are highly reproducible.</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 13.5pt; line-height: 150%; text-align: justify;"><span style='font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: "Verdana","sans-serif"; color: black;'>Solving Equation (1) for α:</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 13.5pt; line-height: 150%; text-align: justify;"> </p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 0.0001pt; line-height: 150%; text-align: justify;"><span style='font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: "Verdana","sans-serif"; color: black;'>                                      α = (R<sub>100 </sub>- R<sub>0</sub>) /( R<sub>0</sub> *(t-t<sub>0</sub>))                                                (3)</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 13.5pt; line-height: 150%; text-align: justify;"><span style='font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: "Verdana","sans-serif"; color: black;'>Where,<br/>
<br/>
t = 100<sup>°</sup>C &amp; t<sub>0 </sub>= 0<sup>°</sup>C<br/>
<br/>
For platinum, α = 0.00385 Ω/Ω/°C <br/>
<br/>
The relationship between resistance and temperature of RTD can be approximated by the Callendar-Van Dusen equation which is given by,</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 13.5pt; line-height: 150%; text-align: justify;"> </p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 13.5pt; line-height: 150%; text-align: justify;"><span style='font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: "Verdana","sans-serif"; color: black;'>                 R<sub>t</sub> / R<sub>0 </sub>= 1+ α [ t – δ((t/100)-1)(t/100) –β((t/100)-1)(t/100)<sup>3</sup>]                      (4)</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 13.5pt; line-height: 150%; text-align: justify;"><span style='font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: "Verdana","sans-serif"; color: black;'>Where,<br/>
<br/>
t = temperature (<sup>°</sup>C)<br/>
R<sub>t </sub>= Resistance at temperature, t<sup>°</sup>C <br/>
R<sub>0</sub> = Resistance at 0<sup>°</sup>C<br/>
α = Constant ( 0.00385Ω/Ω/°C )<br/>
<br/>
δ &amp; β are Linearization coefficients, where β = 0 for t &gt; 0<sup>°</sup>C<br/>
<br/>
The Callendar Van Dusen Equation is used in the standard IEC 751 which is the most popular standard used for RTD. The equation for RTD given in IEC 751 is:</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 13.5pt; line-height: 150%; text-align: justify;"> </p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 0.0001pt; line-height: 150%; text-align: justify;"><span style='font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: "Verdana","sans-serif"; color: black;'>                                 R<sub>t </sub>=R<sub>0</sub> [1+ At+ Bt<sup>2</sup> +C(t-100)t<sup>3</sup> ]                                             (5)</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 0.0001pt; line-height: 150%; text-align: justify;"><span style='font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: "Verdana","sans-serif"; color: black;'>Where,<br/>
A,B,C are constants and C = 0 for t &gt; 0<sup>°</sup>C.<br/>
<br/>
The values of A,B and C for α = 0.00385 are<br/>
A = 3.9083*10<sup>-3</sup>°C‾<sup>1</sup><br/>
B = -5.775*10<sup>-7</sup>°C‾<sup>2</sup><br/>
C = -4.183*10<sup>-12</sup>°C‾<sup>4 </sup><br/>
<br/>
Platinum RTDs are defined by standards such as DIN 43760 (BS1904), IEC 751-1983, and JIS C1604.</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 0.0001pt; line-height: 150%; text-align: justify;"> </p>

<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: 150%;"><strong><span style='font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: "Verdana","sans-serif"; color: black;'>2.</span></strong><span style='font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: "Verdana","sans-serif"; color: black;'> <strong>Accuracy</strong>:It is the comparison of measurement with standard results.That is how good the data is when compared with a recognized standard. The accuracy of a measurement system is the degree of closeness of measurements of a quantity to its actual (true) value.</span><span style='font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: "Verdana","sans-serif";'><br/>
<br/>
<strong><span style="color: black;">3</span></strong><span style="color: black;">. <strong>Stability</strong>: It is the measurement of drift over time. Stability is affected by sensor design. Well designed, high quality sensor will have less drift while high vibration, mechanical wear and tear and thermal shock will degrade the stability.</span><br/>
<br/>
<strong><span style="color: black;">4.</span></strong><span style="color: black;"> <strong>Interchangeability</strong>: Interchangeability is the measure of change in base tolerance and temperature coefficient from sensor to sensor. This is important when replacing a sensor, to insure that the system retains its performance characteristics without any need for re-calibration. </span><br/>
<br/>
<strong><span style="color: black;">5</span></strong><span style="color: black;">. <strong>Response time</strong>: Response time is time the sensor takes to react to temperature changes in a process.</span><br/>
<br/>
<strong><span style="color: black;">Factors that influence the sensor response:</span></strong><br/>
<br/>
<span style="color: black;">• Thermal lag  when combined with the thermowell </span><br/>
<span style="color: black;">• Immersion length</span><br/>
<span style="color: black;">• Flow velocity of the process fluid </span><br/>
<span style="color: black;">• Process fluid transients </span><br/>
<span style="color: black;">• Fluid boundary or film on sensing surface </span><br/>
<span style="color: black;">• Influences from wall or vessel </span><br/>
<span style="color: black;">• </span><span style="color: black;">Environmental factors</span><br/>
<br/>
<strong><span style="color: black;">6. Insulation resistance</span></strong><span style="color: black;">: It is the largest cause of errors and failure of RTD. The causes can be an inadequate seal, high humidity environment, or a failed seal. In some cases baking the RTD assembly will increase the insulation resistance and recover the RTD. </span><br/>
<br/>
<strong><span style="color: black;">7. Self heating</span></strong><span style="color: black;">: The very small cross-sectional areas in the sensing elements will tend to heat when electrical current is applied.
